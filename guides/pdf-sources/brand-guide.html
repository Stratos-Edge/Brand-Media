Logo & Identity.

The Stratos Edge wordmark and lettermark — construction, usage, and rules.

Wordmark construction

Set in Work Sans with a vertical separator between STRATOS and EDGE. STRATOS is weight 500, letter-spacing 0.18em. EDGE is weight 300, letter-spacing 0.14em, opacity 85%. The separator is a 1px × 13px bar at 25% white (on dark) or 15% black (on light) opacity.

On light backgrounds — default for documents
On dark backgrounds — Playbook covers, footers

Clear space and don'ts

Minimum clear space on all sides equals the cap height of the letterforms. Never crop into that space, never distort proportions, never recolor, never add drop-shadows or effects, never replace the fonts, and never embed the wordmark inside filled containers or colored circles.

Lettermark

A compact mark for tight contexts where the full wordmark won't fit: favicons, app icons, profile photos, watermarks. Carries the signature coral period device.

On light backgrounds
On dark backgrounds
Don't pair them
Never use the wordmark and the lettermark together in the same composition. Pick one based on available space.

Typography.

Two fonts. Work Sans is the brand voice; Lora is the body and the editorial moment.

Work Sans — the brand voice
AI failures aren't technology failures.
Google Fonts — Work Sans. Weights: 200, 300, 400, 500, 600, 700. Fallback: -apple-system, BlinkMacSystemFont, "Segoe UI", sans-serif. Brand voice lives at weight 400 for headlines, 200 for serene / large treatments (covers, FlightPlan auth).
Lora — body & editorial
Stratos Edge advises mid-market organizations in healthcare and financial services where getting AI right is a strategy problem before it is a technology problem.
Google Fonts — Lora. Weights: 400, 500, 600, 700 + italic variants. Fallback: Georgia, "Times New Roman", serif. Used for body paragraphs, long-read content, and FlightPlan deliverable hero titles.

Pairing rule

Structure → Work Sans. Story → Lora. The website's one notable inversion: nav is Lora, not Work Sans — reads "publication," not "tech product." Differentiation comes through weight and italic style, not new colors.

The coral period.

The single most signature typographic move. Every brand headline ends with a coral period.

Examples
What we help you do.
FlightPlan™ is how we do it.
Your documents are on the way.
Ready to see the full picture?

Mechanics

The headline is set in INK / white / on-background color; only the terminal . switches to CORAL. The period inherits the headline's font weight and size — it just changes color.

What we help you do<span style="color: #FF6B4A">.</span>

Rules

  • One per headline. Not per sentence in a paragraph.
  • Always at the end. Never mid-headline.
  • Question marks and exclamation marks follow the same rule — the terminal punctuation switches color.
  • The period inherits the headline's font weight and size automatically — don't restyle it.
  • Don't use on body paragraphs. The coral period is a headline device.
  • Don't use more than once per visible page section. It loses meaning if repeated.
Why this matters
The coral period is the cheapest, fastest brand recognition device. Anyone who's seen the website recognizes it. Use it on every document headline so a Stratos Edge document is instantly identifiable — even from across the room.

Backgrounds & rhythm.

A small set of section backgrounds, alternated for rhythm without introducing new colors.

WHITE · #FFFFFF
Default page background. Hero sections. The "rest" between active sections.
OFFWHITE · #F8F8F8
Alternating sections for rhythm. Stat blocks. Subtle differentiation without color shift.
CREAM · #F2F0E9
Warm editorial moments. Long-read content. Pull quotes.
DARK TEAL · #04242D
Hero bands, footers, FlightPlan section dividers, intentional drama. Always with white / coral text.

Rotation

A document that rotates WHITE → OFFWHITE → DARK TEAL → WHITE feels Stratos without any other styling work. Don't add a fifth background.

Page structure

All branded PDFs are 8.5 × 11 in (US Letter) with 0.7in horizontal margins (Playbook) or 0.85in (Simple — more generous, reads more editorial). Content padding starts 0.55in from the top (Playbook cover) or 0.85in (Simple). Footers absolute-positioned at the bottom with a 1pt hairline rule above on Simple docs.

Source of truth
The live homepage at stratosedge.ai and the FlightPlan deliverables. If anything in this guide conflicts with what the site actually does, the homepage wins — this guide is updated to match.
